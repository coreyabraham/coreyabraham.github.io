Project Details

Game Engine: Unity

Platform: PC // Windows 7+ x64

Role(s): Systems and UI Programmer

Development Date: August the 19th, 2024 - December the 4th, 2024

Brief

Scurry is a 2.5D Puzzle Platformer developed with the Unity Engine that has you playing as a rat as you attempt to find salvation.

We had a lot of discussions as a collective team in regards to how this title was going to be produced and developed, as a lot of us in the earlier stages of developemnt didn't exactly know what this game would look and feel like. In the end, we decided on 2D movement within a 3D environment since that would've been the easiest to convey to the Player without having to attribute for other factors that could've slown down the production of the game by a lot.

The remains of this system, along with other tested features, are mostly still intact within the game's files themselves and can be found via the GitHub Repository linked at the bottom of this page, most of the older branches have these prototype assets still burried within the Assets directory!

Contributions

We had quite a ways to go before the game could be seen in our eyes as "complete", as such, there's natrually a lot to show, so let's start off with the basics;

Our team consisted of only two programmers, myself and Josh, as such we had a lot of work to do in order to make the game feel "complete" in a way and to start doing that, we of course created a Technical Design Document for the game. Just like Lights Out, we have an online version of the TDD for anyone to read through to get a better idea of how the game would've been developed across it's runtime.

TDD_TeamMischief_0.1

Right after we wrote the TDD, it was time for us to prototype, in the very early stages of development, the team discussed having a Player Controller and a Camera that had programming to flip the Player's perspective in a 2.5D plane. Seeing as the game was to be created in 3D, we wanted to experiment with the way that the Player interacts with the world, something with a little more flavor instead of going with the regular 2D controls a 2.5D game would usually adopt. However, it turned out that, amongst several discussions, this functionality would soon be cut due to it being too hard to execute on a design level, however during the development of said features, I wrote a starting Player Controller and Camera System which would be the basis for our game. Here's what it looked like during the Alpha stage;

With this in mind, we decided to stick with the 2D movement in 3D environemnt, traditional 2.5D experience and started refining the systems enough for a proper Alpha build.

By the time Alpha had finished, we had a servicable 2.5D Player and Camera System in place, here's the portions I ended up doing for the Player and Camera Systems during this time;

As discussed with the Designers and Artists of the project, we wanted a Player system that really emphasised how small you are in comparison to the environment you're within and to do that, we needed a more robust Player System, which is where my contributions come in. During the prototyping phase I threw together a basic movement system using Unity's built in CharacterController Component, then wrapped all inputs with the newer Input System Package Unity designed specifically to make multi-platform inputs less of a hassle and finally, I then added basic physics to the player character to make sure they had proper weight to them when jumping about.

With the Player System out of the way, we needed a functioning Camera system that tracked the Player's movement, as such I started the task of building one from scratch using Unity's Camera Component system, at first things went surprisingly well, however, with time, comes new additions, which results in more time being sunk into the system to then add onto it and improve it as a whole, this system ended up being the most challenging component I worked on for the game and caused many issues during the development process, at points we even considered switching to Cinemachine, a Unity Package that's widely known for it's vast Camera functionality over the stock Camera.

However, this proved to be unnecessary, as I got my act together and was able to finally fix most of the Camera's growing pains from the early Prototype days, thus paving the way for the Beta - Gold additions, which proved necessary considering how thorough the additions would later become.

Note: The `ZoneCamera.cs` logic later on was written by me and Ethan, an Artist who ended up helping with the math and code-design behind the Zone Camera triggers and their intergration with the `CameraSystem.cs` C# Script, everything else within both Scripts however was written myself.

During this time, we were going for a more "realistic" feel to the rat, which was quickly changed come the Beta phase of the game, mainly due to our team having Play Testers who were quick to point out how, in a 2.D Platform based game, it felt slightly too restrictive in movement, more specifically air control and jumping between platforms within the levels themselves, it felt too imprecise, so with the help of Josh, we both reworked it heavily for the following builds to come after;

Another major part I had during the game's development was unfortunately scrapped due to time constraints and how riddled with bugs it ended up being by the time the project had wrapped up development, that being the Data Systems the game originally used. See, back in the Alpha and Beta builds of the game, we originally had the intention of creating a multitude of levels that the Rat would be tasked to go through, ultimately, we ended up with two main levels in a sort of "Demo" fashion, however, due to this difference in ideas, I had proposed the idea of there being Data Saving in the game which included the two main points;

• Save Files System

• Checkpoint System
